\subsection{Motivation}

Research language is not neutral. Abstracts show what a field thinks is important, what counts as a valid contribution, and what claims need support. As AI, machine learning, and NLP have grown from small academic areas into technologies that affect daily life, the language used to describe this research has changed too, but how to measure that change and which tools best capture it remain unclear.\\

Earlier research in computational scientometrics has examined citation networks, co-occurring keywords, and topic patterns to track changes in the field \citep{chavalarias2013,krenn2020}. These studies usually use a single way of representing data and a single method of comparison, making it hard to distinguish real changes in the discussion from errors introduced by the method. For example, a rise in how often certain words are used could indicate a genuine shift in ideas, a trend in language, or just a shift in where papers are published. Results that appear across different methods with very different assumptions are much more reliable.

\subsection{Research Questions}

This study examines three interconnected research questions. First, how have AI, ML, and NLP research summaries shifted their focus from basic theory to real-world uses between 1990 and 2025? Second, have the ways limitations are described in summaries changed, and has the growth of AI systems in use brought new ethical concerns? Third, how does specific technical language spread in a research field, mainly by how often it is used, or by changing meaning in new discussions? A fourth overall question drives the mixed-method approach: do different text analysis methods agree on when and how these changes happened, or do they find very different patterns?

\subsection{Corpus and Motivating Observation}

The study is based on 24,677 arXiv abstracts, mainly from the cs category.LG, cs.CL and cs.AI, covering the years 2000 to 2021. The number of publications is highly uneven, ranging from 261 abstracts from 2000 to 2004 to 7,677 in 2020 to 2021. The focus of the categories changes from mostly cs.CL in the early years to mostly cs.LG after 2015. This means the collection is not a uniform sample of AI research over time but a changing mix of different subfields, which any analysis must clearly consider.

The extent of language change can be clearly shown. A typical abstract from 2003 says: \textit{``We present a Bayesian model for word sense disambiguation that infers posterior sense distributions from prior lexical knowledge and contextual co-occurrence statistics.''} These two texts use almost no common words and make very different knowledge claims. The first explains a theoretical model, while the second shows test results. Carefully measuring this change across 24,677 documents is the main challenge of this paper.

\subsection{Why a Multi-Method Design}

No single text analysis method can address all aspects of this problem simultaneously. Keyword analysis is easy to understand, but it only works with a fixed set of words. Topic modeling finds hidden themes without requiring predefined words, but it relies on simple word counts and requires manual labeling. Dimensionality reduction works regardless of data type but merges differences within the same year and is affected by changes in subfield makeup. Distributional drift analysis separates how often words are used from changes in their meaning, but it requires careful matching of time periods. Supervised classification measures changes over time directly, but it confuses topic words with the specific signals it aims to detect. Importantly, these weaknesses balance out because each method's strengths compensate for others' limitations. This is why we use five methods here. A result that appears independently across several methods is much more reliable than one found in a single analysis.
